**1. Emerging Trends in Agriculture**

Traditional crop and livestock production centers on people. Breeding, irrigation, fertilization, feeding, disease control, transportation, and sales all relied heavily on accumulated experience and craftsmanship. The result was low efficiency, large swings in output, and little control over quality. The old formula of simply adding labor, land, or inputs can no longer solve agriculture’s challenges. Decisions must shift from people to data so that production can be planned, governed, and executed with precision.

Land transfers, farm consolidation, and the rise of new agricultural operators have created fertile ground for applying AI-powered innovations in agriculture.

**2. What Is Digital Agriculture?**

In recent years, academia has proposed concepts such as smart agriculture, precision agriculture, facility agriculture, and data-driven agriculture. Despite different names, they share several traits:

1. Build intelligent models that incorporate experimental data, historical production experience, knowledge representation, and reasoning techniques to leverage agronomists’ expertise.
2. Use agricultural sensors to collect real-time data on environmental parameters.
3. Feed massive data streams into intelligent models to analyze, mine, predict, and visualize insights so managers can make higher-quality decisions.

Among all these concepts, “digital agriculture” is the easiest to grasp. Guided by green development principles, it treats digital information as a new production factor, deeply integrating the internet, IoT, cloud computing, big data, AI, and smart equipment with modern agriculture. Digital agriculture delivers full-process sensing, quantitative decision-making, intelligent control, precise inputs, and personalized services. It dramatically improves efficiency across the agricultural value chain and optimizes resource allocation. Within the digital economy, it is a textbook case of transforming traditional industries through digital reconfiguration.

**3. A Vast Market for Digital Technology**

Huawei’s _Connected Farm Smart Agriculture Market Assessment_ estimates that the potential market size for digital agriculture will nearly double from USD 13.8 billion in 2015 to USD 26.8 billion in 2020, a 14.24% compound annual growth rate.

**4. The Digital Economy Becomes a Growth Engine**

Leading countries in digital agriculture all prioritize agricultural technology while tailoring strategies to local conditions. According to the China Academy of Information and Communications Technology’s _G20 Digital Economy Development Report (2017)_, the share of added value contributed by the agricultural digital economy exceeded 10% in the UK, Germany, South Korea, the United States, Japan, and France in 2016. The UK ranked first at 25.1%, followed by Germany (21.3%) and South Korea (14.7%). China ranked ninth with more than 5%.

**4.1. United States: Natural Advantages Plus Advanced Tech**

The United States is both the most advanced agricultural country and the world’s largest exporter of farm products. Agriculture accounts for about 1.2% of GDP. As early as the 1980s, the U.S. pioneered the notion of precision agriculture and built AGNET, the largest agricultural network system in the world. AGNET spans 46 states, six Canadian provinces, and seven other countries. It links the USDA, 15 state agriculture departments, 36 universities, and countless agribusinesses to deliver real-time information. Some 41.6% of family farms, 46.8% of dairy farms, and 52% of young farmers go online for agricultural information, supported by specialized service organizations.

Technological progress is the main driver of U.S. agricultural productivity. Today farmers regularly deploy robots, temperature and humidity sensors, aerial imagery, and GPS to boost yields. Revenue continues to rise even with largely flat costs, giving American farms strong competitiveness.

**4.2. Germany: Advanced Machinery Speeds the Digital Transition**

Germany is one of the EU’s top agricultural producers and the world’s third-largest exporter of farm and food products. With highly mechanized farming dominated by small and medium family operations, productivity gains and scientific advances have led to continual consolidation. Germany was also the first to launch “Industry 4.0,” whose core ideas mirror digital agriculture. Both rely on IoT, big data, and cloud technologies to collect data via sensors, process it in digital platforms, and push instructions back to machinery. Digital agriculture further raises Germany’s efficiency.

The German government treats digital agriculture as a priority. Large enterprises spearhead R&D, and according to the Mechanical Engineering Industry Association, investments in agricultural technology reached EUR 5.4 billion in 2016. As part of the EU, Germany also benefits from the “Digitizing European Industry” strategy, which promotes digital upgrades across traditional sectors. Corporations such as Bayer develop intelligent machinery, digital farm management platforms, and end-to-end transformation services that provide farmers with holistic digital solutions.

**4.3. Israel: Scarcity Spurs Innovation**

Israel suffers from scarce freshwater and erratic rainfall; two-thirds of the land is semi-arid or arid, and 80% of food was imported in the early years of statehood. After decades of modernization, domestic production now covers 95% of demand. Scarcity forced the country to improve efficiency, unleashing digital agriculture and big-data applications. Government agencies use macro-level policies to support agritech innovation, including funding R&D and new ventures. Reliable data underpins resource decisions, so technologies around irrigation, automation, mechanization, and informatization flourished. Agriculture has become Israel’s most productive major sector since 2000, outpacing manufacturing, transport, commerce, and construction.

“Saving and efficiency” encapsulate Israel’s approach. Farmers, acutely aware of food security, climate risks, and dwindling water resources, fully embrace modern technology to stretch every drop of water, hectare of land, and hour of labor. Thermal imagers, sensors, drones, satellites, and probes monitor fields around the clock, sending data directly to growers. Faster responses minimize damage from extreme weather and maximize yields.

**5. China’s Opportunity in the Era of Rural Revitalization and 5G**

_5.1 Current Status: Digital Adoption Lags_

The CAICT’s _China Digital Economy Development and Employment White Paper (2019)_ shows that digital output accounts for 7.3% of China’s agricultural economy, compared with 18.3% in industry and 35.9% in services. Forestry, fishery, farming, and animal husbandry all trail most industrial and service sectors, leaving significant room for improvement.

_5.2 Policy Tailwinds_

China has consistently tilted policy support toward agricultural informatization. Since the State Council’s 2011 “Twelfth Five-Year Plan for Agricultural and Rural Informatization,” every subsequent No.1 Central Document has highlighted agricultural modernization. The 2014 document explicitly called for building full-process informatization and mechanization centered on agricultural IoT and precision equipment. Policies accelerated after 2015, underscoring the urgency of digital transformation.

In July 2017, the State Council released the _Next Generation AI Development Plan_, calling for intelligent agriculture pilot projects, including smart farms, plant factories, ranches, fisheries, orchards, processing workshops, and green supply chains. The national “13th Five-Year Plan for Agricultural and Rural Informatization” set a target for IoT and other IT applications to exceed 17% of production activities by 2020, with a compound growth rate of 10.8%.

**6. Commercial Applications and Case Studies**

_6.1. Design Breeding_

Seeds are the strategic high ground in global agriculture. Design breeding blends genetics with bioinformatics, big data, and AI, making molecular design the main battleground. By combining genetic testing and variation data, breeders can rapidly identify trait genes and predict phenotypes. Gene editing rewires synthetic circuits to add new stress resistance or yield traits, while AI helps assemble optimal allele combinations for quantitative trait loci.

_6.2. Crop Management_

Hangzhou Yunhe Zhilian builds grower-centered services that fuse agriscience with digital toolkits. It offers crop- and market-based playbooks that help farmers balance time and resources, lower costs, and boost comprehensive productivity. IoT and AI enable integrated systems for environmental monitoring, crop modeling, and precise control so farms can automate scheduling, monitoring, operations, and differentiated production. In management, big data and AI support digital decision-making to raise efficiency while maintaining both yield and quality.

_6.3. Agricultural Robots_

- **Grafting robots:** Crops such as watermelon and tomato often suffer from soil-borne diseases, making grafting essential for low-cost continuous cultivation. Japan’s TGR Grafting Technology Research Institute built intelligent robots that can detect viable and defective seedlings, skipping the latter and achieving a 98% success rate.
- **Weeding robots:** Weeds compete for space and nutrients and host pests. Herbicide overuse compacts soil and breeds resistance, while manual weeding is impractical. Vision-guided robots segment soil and plants, locate targets, and remove weeds continuously.
- **Harvesting robots:** Manual fruit picking is expensive and labor shortages peak during harvest. Belgium’s OCTINION developed a strawberry-picking robot based on the Dribble autonomous platform. It navigates greenhouses without retrofits, uses computer vision to judge ripeness, and completes a pick every three seconds with quality comparable to skilled workers.
- **Autonomous tractors:** In 2016, Case IH upgraded the Magnum T8 high-horsepower tractor into an autonomous machine. Using radar, lidar, and cameras, it detects obstacles, plans routes, collaborates with conventional equipment, and returns to base for refueling or seeding before running empty—all under remote supervision.
- **Seeding robots:** David Dorhout from Iowa created Prospero, an intelligent seeding robot that collects soil data, computes optimal densities, and sows automatically. Multiple Prosperos can work in formation to maximize efficiency.

_6.4. Pest and Disease Detection_

Monsanto and DataRobot co-developed a computer-vision algorithm that identifies pests and diseases with 95.7% accuracy, far surpassing human experts. Canadian firm Resson uses AI to monitor infestations, analyze trends, and issue early warnings. Hangzhou Yunhe Zhilian outfits UAVs with multispectral sensors to capture vegetation data and classify plant health; a 30-minute flight can scan 300 mu (20 hectares) with 95% accuracy.

_6.5. Nondestructive Testing of Produce_

Image-processing techniques analyze external features and changes in internal structures to evaluate quality without damaging fruit. They measure size, shape, color, and other physical parameters to grade agricultural products.

_6.6. Plant Factories_

Plant factories collect real-time greenhouse data via IoT and apply big data plus AI for intelligent control and precise fertilization. This boosts yields, improves quality, saves labor, and raises profitability. Future long-term missions on Mars will also require intelligent plant factories to supply food.

_6.7. Livestock Farming_

Canada’s Cainthus uses on-farm cameras and computer vision to analyze dairy cows’ faces, emotions, and health conditions. Dutch startup Connecterra combines wearable sensors with fixed detectors to monitor herd health and estrus cycles.

**7. Challenges Facing Agricultural AI**

_7.1. Weak Rural Infrastructure_

Global rural networks deliver less than 20% of urban performance. Many fields lack stable mobile signals, making it hard to deploy IoT devices and undermining AI projects. Sanctions that restrict affordable telecom gear, such as the U.S. ban on Huawei, further slow rural connectivity and intelligent agriculture.

_7.2. Limited Agricultural Data_

Most AI deployments focus on industrial automation, smart cities, or education—areas with high commercialization and returns. Few companies invest heavily in agricultural AI because success depends on massive datasets, iterative labeling, and repeated training. Crop cycles yield usable data only once per year, and building biological knowledge and models can take decades. These factors dampen enthusiasm for agritech R&D.

_7.3. Risks of Applying Lean Methodologies_

Lean production emphasizes rapid adaptation to user needs. Agriculture, however, is shaped by geography, climate, soil, pests, biodiversity, and complex microbial environments. A model that works in one location may fail elsewhere, forcing teams to constantly recalibrate algorithms. That demands cross-industry collaboration with agronomists. Farmers are also reluctant to risk new technology on their land; they prefer to see proven results before scaling up. Consequently, “launch fast, scale fast” is ill-suited to agricultural AI.

**8. Outlook for AI in Agriculture**

Agricultural AI will focus on four areas:

1. **Big-data intelligence:** Combining data mining with knowledge-driven approaches to extract hidden patterns from attribute and spatial data, enabling precise farming strategies.
2. **Cross-media intelligence:** As information shifts from single to multi-media formats, cross-media analysis becomes critical. Machine vision is the core, improving spectral, video, and image processing for better pest diagnostics.
3. **Swarm intelligence:** Deep interconnection between people, big data, and IoT will reshape the cyber-physical world, boosting e-commerce, safety traceability, and logistics for farm products.
4. **Hybrid augmented intelligence:** Because many human problems remain uncertain and open-ended, machines cannot fully replace humans. Incorporating human cognition into AI systems—“human-in-the-loop” approaches—will propel autonomous driving, agricultural robotics, and more.
